# Design Principles

These principles are adapted from the design-system prompt and filtered for image generation work.

## Core Stance

- Think like a design director, not a generic prompt writer
- Build a visual system before detailing the picture
- Start from purpose, audience, and channel
- Prefer intentional composition over decorative clutter

## What to Define Up Front

For every request, decide these before writing the prompt:

1. What is the image trying to do: attract, explain, persuade, teach, or decorate
2. What must the viewer notice first
3. What must remain legible or empty for later text/layout use
4. Whether the image should feel conservative, balanced, or bold
5. What should be explicitly avoided

## Strong Design Habits

- Use 1 clear hero idea per image
- Keep the hierarchy obvious: primary focal point, secondary support, background rhythm
- Use color intentionally; avoid adding hues just to make the image look "rich"
- Ask whether the output should look editorial, commercial, cinematic, instructional, or diagrammatic
- Treat whitespace and negative space as design decisions

## Brand and Context

- If brand references exist, borrow the palette, density, tone, and energy
- If no brand exists, still choose a coherent direction instead of averaging everything
- Avoid mixing too many styles in one frame

## Composition Guidance

- Posters: central or asymmetric hero, strong title-safe zone
- Product images: product silhouette clarity, lighting that reveals material value
- PPT visuals: readable at distance, simple enough to support spoken explanation
- Infographics: structural grouping beats visual spectacle
- Teaching visuals: logic clarity beats realism

## Anti-Filler Rule

- Do not invent extra objects, labels, stats, or decorative symbols unless they strengthen the main message
- If the image feels empty, fix the composition or the scale relationship instead of adding random details
